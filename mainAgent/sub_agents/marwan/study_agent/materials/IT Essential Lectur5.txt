Network History  
============================================================  

1950s–1960s:  
• Early experimental military networks.  
• Development of ARPANET.  
• Introduction of packet-switching technology.  

1970s:  
• Introduction of Ethernet (local networks).  
• Development of TCP/IP protocols.  
• Creation of USENET (early social networking form).  

1980s:  
• ARPANET adopted TCP/IP.  
• NSFNET expanded connectivity to universities and research centers.  

1990s:  
• World Wide Web introduced by Tim Berners-Lee.  
• Graphical web browsers made internet user-friendly.  
• Commercial ISPs emerged.  
• Search engines such as Google improved information retrieval.  

2000s:  
• Broadband and Wi-Fi became widespread.  
• Social media and smartphones increased connectivity.  

2010s:  
• Growth of Cloud Computing.  
• Expansion of IoT (Internet of Things).  
• Development of 5G technology.  

2020s:  
• Focus on 5G, edge computing, AI integration, and IoT expansion.  
• Continued improvements in speed, security, and global connectivity.  


============================================================  
What is a Network?  
============================================================  

A network is a collection of interconnected devices such as computers, servers, printers, and smartphones that communicate to share data, resources, and services.  

Networks can range from small local networks (LAN) to global networks (Internet).  

Networks may be:  
• Wired (using cables)  
• Wireless (using radio waves)  

Networks enable:  
• File sharing  
• Printer sharing  
• Application access  
• Communication services  


============================================================  
5.1 Network Components and Types  
============================================================  

Principles of Networking:  
• Networks are systems formed by links.  
• Examples of everyday networks:  
  – Mail delivery system  
  – Telephone system  
  – Public transportation system  
  – Corporate computer networks  
  – The Internet  

Computer Data Network:  
• Collection of hosts connected by networking devices.  
• Devices include computers, printers, scanners, smartphones, servers.  

Network Connections:  
• Copper cabling  
• Fiber-optic cabling  
• Wireless connections  

Benefits of Networking:  
• Fewer peripherals required  
• Lower licensing costs  
• Increased communication  
• Centralized administration  
• Reduced file duplication  
• Resource conservation  


Network Components:  

1) Host Devices  
   – Devices that send and receive data (PCs, printers, servers).  

2) Intermediary Devices  
   – Devices between hosts that manage data flow.  

3) Network Media  
   – The physical path for data transmission (cables or wireless).  


============================================================  
5.3 Basic Network Devices  
============================================================  

Network Interface Card (NIC):  
• Connects a computer to a wired or wireless network.  

Repeaters:  
• Regenerate and extend network signals.  

Hub:  
• Receives data on one port and sends it to all other ports.  

Bridge:  
• Divides a network into segments and tracks device locations.  

Modem (Modulator/Demodulator):  
• Connects to the internet via ISP.  
• Converts digital signals to analog and vice versa.  
• Uses RJ-11 connectors.  
• Can be internal or external.  

Switch:  
• Records MAC addresses of connected devices.  
• Forwards data based on destination MAC address.  
• Types:  
  – Managed switch (used in companies)  
  – Unmanaged switch (used in homes/small offices)  

Wireless Access Point (AP):  
• Provides wireless network access within limited range.  

Router:  
• Connects different networks.  
• Uses IP addresses to forward traffic.  
• May include switching and wireless features.  

Firewall:  
• Protects network from unauthorized access.  
• Uses Access Control Lists (ACLs) to allow or deny traffic.

5.4 Network Cables  
============================================================  

Network Tools:  

• Wire cutters – Trim wires.  
• Wire strippers – Remove outer cable jacket.  
• Crimper – Attach RJ-45 connectors.  
• Punch down tool – Terminate wires into patch panels.  
• Multimeter – Check voltage and electrical continuity.  
• Cable tester – Test Ethernet cable wiring (T568A/T568B).  
• Wi-Fi analyzer – Analyze wireless signal strength and interference.  
• Tone generator – Trace and identify cables.  
• Loopback adapter – Test network ports and NIC functionality.  


Cable Types:  
• Twisted-pair  
• Coaxial  
• Fiber-optic  


------------------------------------------------------------  
Coaxial Cable  
------------------------------------------------------------  

• Made of copper or aluminum core.  
• Used in cable TV and satellite systems.  
• Harder to install and troubleshoot.  

Types:  
• Thicknet (10Base5) – 500m, 10 Mbps  
• Thinnet (10Base2) – 185m, 10 Mbps  
• RG-59 – Common for cable TV  
• RG-6 – Higher quality, more bandwidth  


------------------------------------------------------------  
Twisted-Pair Cable  
------------------------------------------------------------  

Types:  
• UTP (Unshielded Twisted Pair)  
• STP (Shielded Twisted Pair)  

UTP:  
• Most common  
• Four color-coded wire pairs  
• Prone to EMI and RFI  

STP:  
• Better EMI/RFI protection  
• More expensive  

Wiring Standards:  
• T568A  
• T568B  

Cable Types:  
• Straight-through cable – Same wiring both ends (connect unlike devices).  
• Crossover cable – Different wiring each end (connect like devices).  


Twisted-Pair Categories:  

Category 3 – 100 Mb/s at 100 MHz  
Category 5e – 1 Gb/s at 100 MHz  
Category 6 – 1 Gb/s at 250 MHz  
Category 6a – Up to 500 MHz  

Higher categories provide better performance and EMI protection.  


------------------------------------------------------------  
Fiber-Optic Cable  
------------------------------------------------------------  

• Transmits data using light.  
• Not affected by EMI or RFI.  
• Higher bandwidth and longer distance.  
• More expensive than copper cables.  

Types:  

Single-Mode Fiber (SMF):  
• Small core  
• Uses laser  
• Long distances (hundreds of km)  

Multimode Fiber (MMF):  
• Larger core  
• Uses LEDs  
• Shorter distance (up to 550m)  
• Cheaper
